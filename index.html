Course Info

Description

At their core, websites are boxes within boxes within boxes of information. But, what are websites actually made of, and what can they do? This four-week workshop explores programming as a dynamic digital material, giving rise to unique design languages and possibilities. Students start as programming novices, reading and writing HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to build up foundational skills in web design and development. Alongside hands-on assignments, weekly lectures expose the field of web programming, including topics in computer science, digital design tools and systems (e.g. Figma, Material Design), and professional web development tools (e.g. Webflow, WordPress, Bootstrap, React). Students emerge with portfolio projects and an ability to effectively collaborate with other web developers.

Major elective; Graphic Design majors only.
Open to non-majors with permission by the Department.

What is the point of this course?

Learning Goals

  • To dive into the field of web programming, focusing on HTML/CSS and Javascript/jQuery.
  • To experience the nature of code by designing and programming web pages.
  • To discover a wide range of coding projects for both inspiration and education.
  • To engage with the field of design+technology and see how various technologies and practices work together.

Learning Outcomes

  • Foundational HTML/CSS programming skills and experience.
  • Ability to read, integrate, and edit pre-existing Javascript code.
  • Ability to understand and utilize reference documentation.
  • Research skills for solving technical challenges.
  • Vocabulary to communicate with web developers and programmers in general.
  • Exposure to design and programming tools for digital platforms.

Teaching Philosophy

My coding background is based in computer science. While designers don’t need all that, I hope to expose the most relevant parts of the computer science world. In the professional world, designers usually hand off schematics for programmers to implement. This isn’t a great process — it prevents productive discussion between designers and developers, and can result in products that neither party is fully happy with. With even just a surface-level understanding of how code works, designers can both make design decisions based on sound logic and effectively communicate with developers throughout the whole process.
